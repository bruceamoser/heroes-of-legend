= Core Resolution Mechanics

This is the chapter that makes the game work. Read it once. Bookmark it.
Come back when you need it. Every roll you make -- every sword swing, every
spell, every silver-tongued lie -- runs through the system on this page.

== The Core Roll

Here it is. The whole thing:

*3d6 + Attribute Modifier + Skill Bonus + Difficulty Modifier*

That's it. Roll three dice. Add the relevant number from your character sheet.
Add your skill if you have one. The DA adds or subtracts for difficulty. Look
at the total. Something happens. Always.

[%header,cols="1,1,3"]
|===
| Total | Tier | What Happens
| 1–6 | *Weak* | You succeed, but there's a catch. The door opens -- as the guard
rouses from his nap. Your blade draws blood -- a scratch, not a wound.
The spell fires -- but fizzles at the edges.
| 7–12 | *Standard* | Clean success. Expected outcome. Professional work. This
is what competence looks like -- the lock clicks, the arrow flies
true, the ancient text yields its secrets.
| 13–18+ | *Strong* | You crushed it. The thing you were trying to do? You did
it so well that something extra happens. Extra damage. Bonus
information. The crowd applauds. This is why you trained.
|===

[NOTE]
.Why 3d6? A Designer's Note
====
A single twenty-sided die is a fickle god. Every number from 1 to 20 is
equally likely -- your carefully built character with +7 to Stealth has the
same 5% chance of rolling a 1 as the clumsy ogre in plate mail. That's not
how skill works in stories, and it shouldn't be how it works in games.

Three six-sided dice create a bell curve. Most rolls land between 9 and 12.
That means your training -- your attributes, your skill bonuses, your
Disciplines -- matters *more* than the dice. A master thief almost always
sneaks past the guard. A legendary blacksmith almost never ruins the blade.
When the dice do produce a 3 or an 18, it *means* something -- because it
only happens about once every 200 rolls. This system makes your choices
matter more than your luck. We think that's how it should be.
====

== Attack Rolls: You Always Hit

In *Heroes of Legend*, when you swing your weapon or hurl a spell, *you
connect.* Every time. The question isn't whether you hit -- it's whether you
hit hard enough to matter.

Your 3d6 attack roll determines the damage tier:

*Weak:* Apply the weapon or spell's Weak damage value.
*Standard:* Apply the Standard damage value.
*Strong:* Apply the Strong damage value. Add any critical effects.

Armor reduces incoming damage -- every point of Armor subtracts 1 from the
damage you take (minimum 1 damage from any hit). A goblin with no armor takes
the full 5 from a Strong longsword strike. A knight in plate armor (Armor 3)
would only take 2 from that same blow.

[NOTE]
.Why Always-Hit Makes Better Fights
====
Think about your favorite movie battle. The hero never stands there missing
for six seconds while the audience checks their phones. Every exchange
advances the fight -- a clash, a parry, a glancing blow, a telling strike.
That's what always-hit delivers. Combat moves fast. Every round, something
changes. Your 3d6 roll isn't "do I hit?" -- it's "how much does this hurt?"
====

== Critical Hits (Three Natural 6s)

Three sixes. The dice come up 6, 6, 6. The table erupts. You just rolled a
Critical.

Critical hits are automatic *Strong* results. Then roll a d6 for your bonus:

[%header,cols="1,4"]
|===
| d6 | Critical Effect
| 1 | *Maximum Damage.* Deal the weapon's Strong damage twice.
| 2 | *Devastating Blow.* Target is stunned, prone, or disarmed -- your choice.
| 3 | *Moment of Glory.* Take a bonus action or free move immediately.
| 4 | *Second Wind.* Recover 3 HP or regain one expended ability.
| 5 | *Inspired Strike.* One ally gains advantage on their next attack against this target.
| 6 | *Narrative Flourish.* Describe something epic. The DA makes it matter.
|===

Critical hits happen about once every 216 rolls. When they do, they should
feel like a lightning strike. Let the player describe it. Let the table
celebrate. This is why we play.

== Fumbles (Three Natural 1s)

Snake eyes, but worse. Three 1s. Something has gone spectacularly wrong.

Fumbles are automatic failures. Then roll a d6 for the complication:

[%header,cols="1,4"]
|===
| d6 | Complication
| 1 | *Weapon Trouble.* Drop your weapon or jam your bow. Takes an action to fix.
| 2 | *Friendly Fire.* Nearest ally takes Weak damage from your wild swing.
| 3 | *Off Balance.* The next attack against you has advantage.
| 4 | *Gear Damage.* Armor degrades one step. Shield cracks. Bowstring snaps.
| 5 | *Spell Backfire.* Take your spell's Weak damage yourself. Magic is fickle.
| 6 | *Narrative Twist.* The DA introduces an unexpected complication. Be creative.
|===

Fumbles are rare -- about 1 in 216 rolls, same as criticals. They should be
dramatic, memorable, and occasionally hilarious. A good fumble isn't a
punishment; it's a story beat.

== Difficulty Modifiers

Not every task is created equal. The DA sets a difficulty modifier based on
how hard the thing you're attempting actually is:

[%header,cols="1,3"]
|===
| +4 | Trivial. You've done this a hundred times. Rolling is a formality.
| +2 | Easier than normal. Favorable conditions. You have the high ground.
| +0 | Standard challenge. A fair test of your abilities.
| -2 | Harder than normal. Adverse conditions. It's dark. It's raining. The guards are alert.
| -4 | Very difficult. You're attempting something improbable. Be ready to fail.
| -6 | Near-impossible. The kind of thing bards write songs about -- if you pull it off.
|===

[NOTE]
.When to Roll
====
Only roll when failure is interesting. If the hero has time, tools, and
training, they just succeed -- no roll required. The dice come out when
there's pressure, risk, or a genuine chance of things going sideways.
====

== Worked Examples

*Kael the Blade attacks the goblin chieftain.* Kael's Brawn is +2 and his Blade
Fighting skill is Adept (+2). He rolls 4+3+5=12, then adds +2+2=16. *Strong!*
His longsword deals 5 Strong damage. The chieftain's crude leather gives Armor 1,
so he takes 4 damage. The chieftain roars -- he felt that one.

*Lyra the Intellect identifies an ancient rune.* Knowledge +2, no skill, difficulty -2
(it's very old and partially worn). She rolls 2+4+4=10, adds +2 for Knowledge,
subtracts 2 for difficulty -- total 10. *Standard.* She recognizes it as Old
Elvish -- a warding glyph meant to keep something *in*, not out. That's
concerning.

*Roric the Protector charges the troll. Rolls 6, 6, 6.* The table erupts.
*Critical!* Bonus roll: 4 -- Second Wind, recover 3 HP. Strong damage from his
greataxe: 8. The troll's tough hide (Armor 2) reduces it to 6. Still -- six
damage in one swing, and Roric gets his breath back. The troll's expression
changes from hunger to concern.

*Makeva the Odd tries to pick a lock while guards approach.* Agility +1, Lockpicking
Adept (+2), but it's dark (-2 difficulty) and she's rushing (-2 more). Total
modifier: +1+2-2-2 = -1. She rolls 3+3+5=11, minus 1 = 10. *Standard.* The
lock clicks open just as the guards' torchlight rounds the corner. She slips
through the door and eases it shut behind her. Close. Very close.
