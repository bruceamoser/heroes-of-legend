# Voice Guidelines — Heroes of Legend Rulebook

> **Status:** Approved  
> **Last updated:** 2026-07-12  
> **Applies to:** All rulebook chapters, sidebars, worked examples, and fiction passages

---

## Primary Voice: Battle-Scarred Mentor

**Tagline:** *"Listen up, kid."*

This is the voice of the rulebook. Every mechanical explanation, every worked example, every chapter opening — the Mentor is talking to the reader like a grizzled veteran training a new recruit. The reader is always "you." The Mentor has been through the wars and doesn't have time for fluff.

### Voice Parameters

| Parameter | Rule | Example |
|-----------|------|---------|
| **Person** | Second person ("you") always | "You roll 3d6." NOT "The player rolls 3d6." |
| **Contractions** | Heavy use. Always contract. | "You're," "don't," "it's," "that's," "can't," "won't" |
| **Sentence length** | Short. Punchy. Fragments allowed. | "Three sixes. The table erupts. You just rolled a Critical." |
| **Metaphor domain** | Combat, survival, physical craft | "This is your hammer. Every roll is a nail." "You're the wall." |
| **Tone** | Direct, commanding, impatient with fluff | "Here's how it works." NOT "One might consider the following." |
| **Address** | "You" = the player. "The DA" = the Dungeon Architect. | "The DA sets the difficulty. You roll the dice." |
| **Rhetorical questions** | Used as challenges | "You think you're ready for a Master-tier spell?" |
| **Editorial asides** | Rare, gruff, in parentheses | "(Yeah, I've seen that go wrong. Still worth it.)" |
| **Humor** | Dark, dry, battlefield gallows humor | "Three 1s. Something has gone spectacularly wrong. The table groans. You'll laugh about this later. Much later." |
| **Excitement** | Earned. Reserved for criticals and Strong results. | "This is why we play." |

### Mentor Voice Do's and Don'ts

**DO:**
- Start mechanical explanations with "Here's how it works" or "Here it is"
- Use imperatives: "Roll your dice." "Add your Brawn." "Pick a class."
- Break rules into short, scannable chunks
- Address potential confusion directly: "Yeah, that sounds complicated. It's not. Watch."
- Use physical, visceral language: "Your blade finds the gap." "The spell tears through."

**DON'T:**
- Use passive voice: "The dice are rolled" → "Roll the dice"
- Use academic hedging: "It may be observed that..." → No.
- Use third person for the player: "The player then..." → "You then..."
- Over-explain obvious things
- Use jargon without immediately defining it in plain language

## Secondary Voice: Veteran Adventurer

**Tagline:** *"I've seen a thing or two."*

Used for sidebars, design notes, fiction passages, lore flavor, and the "Design Justifications" section. Same DNA as the Mentor but lower register — boots off the table, drink in hand, swapping stories by the fire.

### Voice Parameters

| Parameter | Rule | Example |
|-----------|------|---------|
| **Person** | First person ("I," "we") | "I've watched a d20 kill more characters than any monster." |
| **Contractions** | Yes, but slightly less aggressive | Same as Mentor but with more reflective pauses |
| **Sentence length** | Medium. Can be more conversational, reflective. | "We tried spell slots for two years. The party wizard spent half the final battle saying 'I'm out of gas.' That's not heroic. That's a scheduling error." |
| **Metaphor domain** | War stories, tavern tales, hard-won wisdom | "Here's what the playtest taught us." |
| **Tone** | Conversational, wry, self-deprecating, honest | "Yeah, this rule exists because my players broke the game. Twice." |
| **Humor** | Dark jokes, ironic observations | "The d20 doesn't care about your character backstory." |
| **Reflection** | Can explain WHY a design decision was made | "We built always-hit because missing isn't fun. It's realistic — but we're telling stories about dragon-slaying wizards. Realism already left the building." |

### When to Use Each Voice

| Context | Voice |
|---------|-------|
| Chapter opening paragraphs | Battle-Scarred Mentor |
| Mechanical explanations | Battle-Scarred Mentor |
| Worked examples | Battle-Scarred Mentor |
| Rules text (tables, bullet lists) | Neutral (clarity first) |
| Sidebar callouts (NOTE, TIP, WARNING) | Battle-Scarred Mentor (brief) or Veteran Adventurer (reflective) |
| Design Justifications / "Why we built it this way" | Veteran Adventurer |
| Fiction passages / chapter epigraphs | Veteran Adventurer |
| GM-facing advice | Battle-Scarred Mentor (direct) with Veteran Adventurer warmth |

## Before and After: Voice Rewrite Example

### Before (Flat Technical Manual)

> "In Heroes of Legend, the core resolution mechanic uses a 3d6 dice pool. Players roll three six-sided dice and add their relevant attribute modifier plus any applicable skill bonus. The Dungeon Architect may also apply a difficulty modifier to reflect how challenging the task is. The total result is compared against three success tiers: Weak (1-6), Standard (7-12), and Strong (13-18+). Results of three natural 6s are considered a Critical Success, while three natural 1s constitute a Fumble."

### After (Battle-Scarred Mentor)

> "Here it is. The whole game in one roll:
>
> **3d6 + Attribute + Skill + Difficulty.**
>
> Grab three dice. Add the number from your sheet that matches what you're doing — Brawn for fighting, Agility for sneaking, Knowledge for remembering. Add your skill if you've got one. The DA adds or subtracts for how hard the thing is. Look at the total. Something happens. Always.
>
> **Weak (1–6):** You pulled it off. Barely. The lock clicks — as the guard rounds the corner.
> **Standard (7–12):** Clean. Professional. This is what competence looks like.
> **Strong (13–18+):** You made it look easy. Extra damage. Bonus information. The crowd applauds.
>
> Three natural 6s is a **Critical** — the table erupts. Three natural 1s is a **Fumble** — something's gone spectacularly wrong. Both happen about once every 200 rolls. When they do, they matter."

## Writer's Checklist: Voice Audit

Before committing any chapter prose, ask:

- [ ] **Contractions:** Are "you are," "do not," "it is," "that is," "cannot," "will not" replaced with "you're," "don't," "it's," "that's," "can't," "won't"?
- [ ] **Second person:** Does the passage address "you" (the player) directly? No "the player," "one," "characters" as subjects?
- [ ] **Sentence length:** Are sentences short and punchy? Can any long sentence be broken into two or three?
- [ ] **Passive voice:** Is "The dice are rolled" rewritten as "Roll the dice"? Is "A check is made" rewritten as "Make a check"?
- [ ] **Opening hook:** Does the chapter or section start with a direct address, a command, or a bold statement — not a preamble?
- [ ] **Metaphors:** Are abstract concepts grounded in physical, combat, or survival imagery?
- [ ] **Rhetorical questions:** Used sparingly (max 1–2 per chapter) as challenges, not filler?
- [ ] **Excitement:** Is excitement earned? Are Criticals and Strong results described with genuine energy?
- [ ] **Sidebars:** If reflective/design-focused, does the sidebar use Veteran Adventurer (first person, conversational, wry)?
- [ ] **Mechanics preserved:** Have all numbers, table values, and cross-references survived the rewrite untouched?

## Voice Intensity by Chapter Type

| Chapter Type | Mentor Intensity | Notes |
|-------------|-----------------|-------|
| Core mechanics (06, 08, 13) | **Maximum** | These are the chapters players reference most. Voice must be clearest here. |
| Character options (03, 04, 05, 07) | **High** | Sell the fantasy. Make them want to play. |
| Magic & spells (10, 11, 12) | **High** | Mentor directness + touch of wonder for magic |
| Equipment (15, 16, 17) | **Medium** | Reference chapters. Clarity first, voice second. |
| GM guidance (19) | **Medium-High** | Direct, but with Veteran Adventurer warmth and honesty |
| Bestiary (20) | **Medium** | Monster entries can be clinical; flavor text should be Veteran Adventurer |
| Reference (21, 22, 23) | **Low** | Clarity is everything. Voice is secondary. |
